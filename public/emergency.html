Emergency Preparedness & Troubleshooting

For a solo senior traveler recovering from cancer, comprehensive emergency preparedness is vital. This section outlines essential services, contacts, and troubleshooting advice to mitigate potential disruptions and ensure a safe journey.

Roadside Assistance & Emergency Contacts

Having robust roadside assistance is paramount for a van of this age. Services like AAA Plus RV or Good Sam Roadside Assistance are highly recommended due to their specialized coverage for recreational vehicles.

Roadside Assistance Providers:

  • AAA Plus RV / Premier RV: These plans offer extended roadside assistance for RVs and motorcycles, including towing benefits (up to 100 or 200 miles depending on the plan), flat tire service, fuel delivery, battery service, mechanical first aid, and lockout service. AAA provides 24/7 nationwide service and often has faster arrival times. The traveler's membership card and photo ID are required for service.
  • Good Sam Roadside Assistance (Platinum/Platinum+ plans): These plans offer unlimited distance towing to the nearest service center, mobile mechanic service, tire service (including roadside tire delivery), fuel/fluid delivery, battery jump-starts, and lockout service. A significant advantage is access to certified RV technicians who can provide operational and technical assistance. Trip interruption assistance is also a valuable benefit for breakdowns far from home.
  • Escapees Roadside Assistance: Another option, available to Escapees members, offers unlimited roadside assistance, including towing to the nearest repair facility, mobile mechanic service, tire changes, fuel delivery, lockout service, battery jump-starts, and winching assistance. They also provide 24/7 technical assistance from RVIA/RVDA and ASE Certified Technicians.

Contacting Roadside Assistance: For AAA, call 1-800-AAA-HELP (1-800-222-4357) or use their digital roadside request tool. For Good Sam and Escapees, refer to their plan benefits for contact numbers.

Emergency Contacts:

  • Personal Emergency Contacts: Reiterate the importance of having a readily accessible list of close family members and friends, her primary doctor's contact information, and local emergency services (911).
  • Pet Emergency Contacts (24-Hour Veterinary Clinics): Identifying emergency veterinary care along the route is crucial for the dogs' well-being.
    • Oklahoma City, OK (near I-35/I-44): Neel Veterinary Hospital
    • Salina, KS (near I-70): Kansas State University Veterinary Health Center (Manhattan, approx. 1 hour east of Salina)
    • Salina, KS: Salina Animal Services
    • North Platte, NE (near I-80): Nebraska Animal Medical Center (Lincoln, approx. 3 hours east)
    • Laramie, WY (near I-80): Animal Health Center
    • Bozeman, MT (near Belgrade, MT): Bridger Veterinary Specialists & Emergency
  • RV Specific Emergency Contacts: For technical issues with the van's conversion systems (e.g., electrical, water, appliances), utilizing the RV-specific expertise offered by Good Sam or Escapees Roadside Assistance can be invaluable. They provide access to RVIA/RVDA and ASE Certified Technicians for troubleshooting and repair referrals.

Common Travel Disruptions & Troubleshooting

Anticipating and preparing for common travel disruptions can significantly reduce stress and ensure a smoother journey.

  • Flat Tires:
    • Preparation: Ensure the van has a good spare tire, a tire repair kit (plug kit), and a portable air compressor.
    • Action: If a flat occurs, assess the situation safely. If comfortable, attempt to change the tire or use the repair kit for a temporary fix. Otherwise, immediately contact roadside assistance.
  • Vehicle Breakdowns:
    • Preparation: Regular pre-trip checks (as outlined in Section II) are the best prevention. Carry basic tools.
    • Action: Pull over to a safe location immediately. Contact roadside assistance (AAA, Good Sam, or Escapees). If the issue is minor (e.g., dead battery, blown fuse), attempt basic troubleshooting if comfortable and safe to do so. Ensure the van's auxiliary battery is charged for emergency lighting and communication.
  • Health Emergencies:
    • Preparation: Adhere strictly to the personal health preparation guidelines (Section II). Wear a medical alert ID.
    • Action: If feeling unwell, pull over safely. If able, contact emergency services (911) and/or her designated emergency contacts. Be prepared to clearly describe symptoms and location.
  • Pet-Related Issues:
    • Preparation: Keep the pet first aid kit easily accessible. Have current vet records (digital and physical) for both dogs. Know the locations and contact information for emergency vets along the route.
    • Action: For minor issues, use the pet first aid kit. For serious concerns, locate the nearest emergency vet clinic and head there immediately, or call for guidance.
  • Weather and Road Conditions:
    • Preparation: Regularly check state DOT websites/apps (KanDrive for KS, NDOT for NE, WYDOT for WY, COTrip for CO) for real-time updates on road closures, construction, and weather alerts.
    • Action: Be flexible with the itinerary. If adverse conditions are reported (e.g., heavy storms, high winds, road closures), delay travel or seek a safe, secure overnight location until conditions improve.
  • Campground Limitations or Pet Restrictions:
    • Preparation: Always call ahead to primary campgrounds to confirm availability and specific pet policies (e.g., breed restrictions, leash rules, designated pet areas).
    • Action: If the primary site is unavailable or has unexpected restrictions, immediately refer to the pre-identified backup overnight options.

Generator Safety Guidelines

If the traveler opts to carry a small inverter generator for emergency power, adherence to strict safety guidelines is critical to prevent carbon monoxide (CO) poisoning and fire hazards.

  • Proper Ventilation and Distance: Generators must always be operated outdoors with sufficient airflow. The Centers for Disease Control (CDC) recommends keeping portable and inverter generators at least 20 feet away from the RV. The exhaust should be directed away from the van and any surrounding RVs, ensuring it is clear of doors, windows, vents, and other openings. This minimizes the risk of CO fumes entering the living space. Obstructions in the exhaust path, even from shelters, must be avoided.
  • Appropriate Fuel Storage: Any backup fuel (gasoline, diesel, or propane) must be stored and transported in approved, sealed fuel containers made of plastic or metal. Fuel should be kept a safe distance from the generator itself and any other heat sources, such as gas or electric stoves, grills, or fire pits. Smoking or lighting cigarettes near the generator or fuel storage is strictly prohibited.
  • Correct Cords and Cables: Only high-rated, low-gauge power cords and cables that can handle the total amperage of the appliances being powered should be used. All cords and cables must be in good condition, free from fraying, breaks, or damage.
  • Carbon Monoxide Detectors: Install battery-operated CO detectors inside the van, and check their functionality before the trip. Pack extra batteries. These detectors provide an early warning if CO levels become unsafe.
